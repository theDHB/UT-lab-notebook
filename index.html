Determining pH with Fisher Acument Model 23A pH/Ion Meter


Temperature should be set on 25 °C. Function should be set on pH.
Refill electrode with pH electrode filling solution as needed to keep it filled up to the hole.

Calibration

1. Remove electrode from storing solution. Slide plastic tubing down to uncover hole on electrode.
2. Rinse electrode with distilled water and blot dry with Kim-wipe. Never touch the tip of the electrode. It is very thin and will easily break.
3. Store electrode temporarily in distilled water if it will be more than about 5 seconds between readings. Always blot electrode before placing it into standard reference solution.
4. Place electrode into beaker of pH 4 reference solution.
5. Move switch from "standby" to ''use".
6. If meter does not read 4.0, use standardize knob to set it on 4.0.
7. Move switch to pH Ref. between readings.
8. Rinse electrode with distilled water and blot dry.
9. Place electrode into beaker of pH 7 reference solution.
10. If meter does not read 7.0, use standardize knob to set on 7.0.
11. Check standards to see that they now read 4.0 and 7.0. If not, repeat steps 4-10.

Reading samples

1. Weigh 20 g soil into cup or beaker.
2. Add 20 ml distilled water to cup.
3. Stir with glass stir rod.
4. Let sit 30 minutes.
5. Stir again and read pH. Make sure solution covers the dot about W' from the tip of the electrode.
6. Rinse electrode between readings.
7. After all readings are completed, return switch to "STBY" for storage.
8. Place electrode into storage solution.
9. Slide plastic tubing up to cover the hole.

Media sterilization

Colonies happily reproduce when fungal spores or bacteria-laden microscopic particles make contact with your plates, broths, and tubes; and your precious media will eventually resemble something out of an abandoned full refrigerator. One can't recognize individual colonies when the plates are covered with fuzz! No untreated surface in the lab is sterile, and nearly all dust and other particles have spores or active cells on their surfaces. All labware and all media must be sterilized before use. We sterilize most media and supplies using a steam autoclave that produces moist heat. Other methods, including filtration, ethylene oxide, radiation, or ultraviolet light may be necessary if components are heat-liable or materials are not heat resistant.

An autoclave is designed to deliver steam into a pressure chamber, generating high heat and pressure at the same time. Heating media to above 121 degrees C for a minimum of 20 min. should destroy all living cells and spores. High pressure (typically 20 lbs/sq in) allows the temperature to exceed 100 degrees without boiling off the water from the solutions being sterilized. We use an autoclave that starts timing when the temperature reaches 121 degrees, and exhausts the steam slowly after the prescribed time above 121 degrees (to prevent exploding bottles!). The autoclave is effectively a giant pressure cooker.


To properly use an autoclave:
  • Know the instrument - some are fully automatic, some are fully manual
  • Prepare supplies properly - the more layers or greater the volume, the longer it will take for the interior to heat up
  • Check the steam pressure and ensure that the instrument is set for slow exhaust if liquids are to be sterilized
  • Ensure that the door is closed properly and securely
  • Check that the time and/or automatic cycle are set properly
  • Ensure that the temperature is well below 100 degrees before attempting to open the door
  • Crack the door to allow steam to vent, keeping face and hands well away from the opening
  • CAUTION! Exposing tightly stoppered bottles to variable pressures invites explosion and injury. When heating any liquids using any method, take care disturbing the flask or bottle. Material near the bottom may be superheated and boil over when moved. Stoppers, caps, covers, must be vented - never make them fit tightly.

Aseptic technique

The media on which you culture desirable microorganisms will readily grow undesirable contaminants; especially molds, other types of fungus, and bacteria from your skin and hair. It is therefore essential that you protect your cultures from contamination from airborne spores and living microorganisms, surface contaminants that may be on your instruments, and from skin contact.

Bacteria and other contaminants cannot fly. Nearly all forms of contamination are carried on microscopic dust particles that make their way onto sterile surfaces when they are carelessly handled. One exception is insect contamination, such as by ants or fruit flies. Fruit flies are a particular nuisance because they can crawl under the lids of agar plates and lay eggs. You would think that people doing genetics research would have developed a model by now that can't fly into other peoples' experiments!

  • Never leave a culture dish open, even for a short time when viewing colonies of organisms, unless you intend to destroy it
  • When it is necessary to open a dish, keep the lid close to the dish, open it only as far and as long as is necessary to accomplish the procedure, and keep the lid between your face (and your germs!) and the agar surface.
  • For most bacterial cultures you will use a sterile loop or needle to inoculate or to obtain an inoculum.
  • Flame a loop or needle to red-hot just prior to use, burning off any organic material
  • Cool the instrument by touching the sterile agar or liquid surface prior to touching a culture (or else you will kill it)
  • Re-sterilize the instrument after performing the procedure, putting down safely without burning the bench, you, or another student
  • Pass the neck of a culture tube or any container with a culture or sterile contents through a flame before taking off the cap. Hold the cap with opening down, and the tube horizontal or nearly so. Convection from the heated neck will prevent dust from falling into the opening. Flame again before putting the cap back (see 'preparing a bacterial smear')
  • Use sterile disposable pipets to remove samples from a broth culture that must be kept uncontaminated.
  • Always be aware of where your hands are,where your face is, and whether or not your culture is in a position to be contaminated. If you have long hair, make sure it does not hang into your plate. Hair is full of potential contaminants, and is one of the principle sources of contaminant microorganisms.
  • If you have an open flame, long hair that is not tied back or loose clothing can be hazardous to your health.
  • Keep flammables away from the flames, including alcohol used for sterilizing instruments. Do not place a heated loop or glass rod into an alcohol dish.

A contaminated culture can often be rescued; however, there is always the risk that you will re­isolate the wrong microorganism. Besides, you don't have that kind of time to waste. Exercise extreme care to keep your cultures pure.

SIUC Method of SDS Scoring

Considerations for Obtaining Meaningful Field SDS Ratings
Site Selection:
To obtain valid comparisons among varieties in a field test, it is essential to have reasonable uniform disease pressure. Therefore, we choose fields with a history of severe SDS occurring uniformly throughout the field in several previous seasons. In addition, we require a SDS test plot to be level or very gently sloped, and to have a uniform soil type. In order to maintain our preferred com-soybean rotation, we scout potential fields at peak SOS expression two years before we want to use them. We have found it important to scout fields ourselves, for what is "bad SDS throughout the field" to a farmer may be woefully variable for a variety test.

Experimental Design:
Reducing Spatial Variability
Because SDS symptom development so strongly depends on the interaction of weather conditions with the stage of plant development, we conduct a separate trial for each one-half maturity group when it is practical. This benefit is two-fold. It allows for better com11arisons within maturity groups and it keeps trials physically small thus reducing spatial variability.

Planting
Since early planting dates have proven to be conducive to disease development, trials are planted as early as possible. Most trials involve 2-row, 10 ft. plots. We consider two rows sufficient since our experience has failed to identify any border effects.
When possible, the land is prepared by no-till practices. If post-emergence weed control is needed, we generally use spot spraying, cultivation and/or hand weeding. To eliminate interference with SOS ratings we rigorously avoid herbicides that cause any soybean leaf damage past the V4 stage. Irrigated locations offer the greatest guarantee of SDS symptoms. However, keep in mind that during a drought year, even irrigation will have a difficult time mimicking natural rainfall.

Rating Leaf Symptoms
Disease symptoms are rated as close as possible to the R6 stage (at which seeds have just reached full size), but before the earliest maturing entries have started to senesce. This is when leaf symptom expression is most developed. When other leaf diseases are prominent, it is sometimes necessary to rate earlier than R6 to avoid masking of SOS by the other diseases. R­ stages are pictured on the following page.
Disease incidence (DI) is the percentage of plants in the plot with leaf symptoms.
Disease severity (DS) is pictured on the following page. It is recorded on a I to 9 scale with increments of 0.5.
Disease index (DX) is calculated as DI x DS/9, and has a range of 0 (no disease) to 100 (all plants dead before R6).

SIUC Method of SDS Scoring

Determining R-stage: All descriptions apply to at least one pod at any of the four uppermost nodes. Other pads at these same nodes may not be fully developed. Stages are determined by seed and pod development on the main stem. By R5.O, the leaf at the terminal node is normally fully developed, so the phrase "with a fully developed leaf" was omitted from the description of the four uppermost nodes.

R-stage Description
5.6 Seed fills 3/4 of lateral surface area of pod cavity. Seed is firm against lateral pod wall at point of maximum seed thickness.
5.8 Seed fills 9/10 of lateral surface area of pod cavity, but does not press against pod suture opposite the hilum.
6.0 Seed fills pod cavity, fully covering membrane, and pressing against pod suture opposite the hilum for at least 1/2 of the length of the seed.
6.2 Seed thickening has caused inner septation walls to separate at least 1 mm (measured by cutting through pod wall at narrowest point of septation) between adjacent seed.
6.4 Inner septation walls are separated by at least 2 mm (measured by cutting as above) between adjacent seed.
6.6 More than 1/4 of the pods are distinctly yellow.

DI = % of plants with leaf symptoms. Record DI in increments of 5.

Record in increments of 0.5. Score ONLY those plants showing symptoms.

    Gram Stain

    1. Perform a bacterial smear, as discussed in Figure 3-52 on page 150 of your lab manual.
    2. Saturate the smear with crystal violet for 1 minute.
    3. Rinse the slide gently with water.
    4. Saturate the smear with iodine for 1 minute.
    5. Rinse the slide gently with water.
    6. Decolorize with Gram decolorizer (acetone/alcohol) for 3-5 seconds ONLY; if you leave the decolorizer on too long, it will bleach the crystal violet out of a gram positive cell!
    7. Rinse the slide gently with water.
    8. Counterstain with safranin for 1 minute.
    9. Rinse the slide gently with water.
    10. Carefully blot the slide dry with bibulous paper.
    11. Observe the slide under the microscope, using proper microscope technique.

    Gram positive bacteria will stain purple.
    Gram negative bacteria will stain red/pink.

The smear should be thin enough to dry completely ·within a few seconds. Stain does not penetrate thickly applied specimens, making interpretation very difficult.
      • Spread the culture with an inoculation loop to an even, thin film over a circle of 1.5 cm in diameter, approximately the size of a dime. Thus, a typical slide can simultaneously accommodate 3 to 4 small smears if more than one culture is to be examined.
      • Air-dry the culture and fix it or over a gentle flame while moving the slide in a circular fashion to avoid localized overheating. The applied heat helps the cell adhere to the glass slide and make possible the subsequent rinsing of the smear without a significant loss of the culture. Heat can also be applied to facilitate drying the smear. However, ring patterns can form if heating is not uniform, e.g. taking the slide in and out of the flame.
    2. Gram Staining:
      • Add crystal violet stain over the fixed culture. Let stand for 10 to 60 seconds; for thinly prepared slides, it is usually acceptable to pour the stain on and off immediately. Pour off the stain and gently rinse the excess stain with a stream of water from a faucet or a plastic water bottle. Note that the objective of this step is to wash off the stain, not the fixed culture.
      • Add the iodine solution on the smear, enough to cover the fixed culture. Let stand for 10 to 60 seconds. Pour off the iodine solution and rinse the slide with running water. Shake off the excess water from the surface.
      • Add a few drops of decolorizer so the solution trickles down the slide. Rinse it off with water after 5 seconds. The exact time to stop is when the solvent is no longer colored as it flows over the slide. Further delay will cause excess decolorization in the gram-positive cell, and the purpose of staining will be defeated.
      • Counterstain with basic fuchsin solution for 40 to 60 seconds. Wash off the solution with water. Blot with bibulous paper to remove the excess water. Alternatively, the slide may be shaken to remove most of the water and air-dried.
    3. Quality control:
      • It is a simple matter to prepare a control slide by breaking a clean wooden applicator stick and picking a small amount of material from the interproximal space of one's teeth. This should be smeared into a drop of clean tap water on a clean glass slide. The slide may be stained as above. This material will consistently display a few neutrophils and a mixture of Gram (+) and (-) organisms. Neutrophil nuclei should be pink.
    4. Examine the finished slide under a microscope.
      • A caveat in the examination of the Gram smears is the distortion in morphology that can be caused by antimicrobial therapy. This is especially likely to occur in urine speciments. Filamentous and pleomorphic forms may be observed among the Gram (-) rod species. Gram reaction of the organism may also change after antimicrobial therapy, Gram (+) bacterial may become gram variable. Look at areas that are one cell thick only; observation of thick areas will give variable and often incorrect results. White blood cells and macrophages should stain Gram-negative, whereas squamous epithelial cells are Gram-positive.

    Preparing Samples

    Soil samples are prepared for counting (quantification of nematode numbers) in three steps, but these steps will vary depending on what kind of analysis we are doing. Numbers in parentheses refer to procedures described on the following pages.

    • Processing, or extracting nematodes from the soil, involves sample screening and set-up (I).
    • Running the samples through the elutriator and transferring samples from sieves to tubes (IIA for egg samples, IIB for verm samples).
    • Getting the sample ready to count, means either grinding and staining egg samples (IIIA) or spinning verm samples in the centrifuge (IIIB).

    The final step is counting the number of SCN eggs per sample (or determining the number and kind of vermiform nematodes in the sample, which is done by Pat.)

    We use the following equipment:
    • screen (hardware cloth) to break up and mix soil.
    • Dishpan or suitable container to catch soil
    • elutriator a machine that floats nematodes out of soil and onto sieves, a small (5") 20 mesh sieve and an 8" 60 mesh sieve to catch cysts while separating out trash
    • a large (8") 400 mesh sieve to catch vermiform nematodes
    • hose and water bottles and funnels to remove samples from sieves
    • sets of beakers to hold samples while awaiting further processing
    • a stopper attached to a drill bit and inserted in a drill press to grind cyst samples and release eggs
    • a 60 mesh screen mounted in a pvc pipe
    • a container of clean water to clean the rubber stopper between samples
    • a small (6") 200 mesh sieve over a small 500·mesh sieve to separate eggs from cyst fragments
    • sets of 150 mL beakers to hold egg samples
    • acid fuschin stain for egg samples
    • microwave to heat egg samples for staining
    • nematode counting slides, disposable pipets, and microscopes to count eggs
    • centrifuge for cleaning up verm samples by concentrating the nematodes in the soil followed by floating the nematode in a sugar solution to separate them from the soil
    • sugar solution for verms: made by dissolving 454 grams (1 pound) sugar in 1 liter of water
    • a small 500 mesh sieve to separate verms from the sugar solution • lab markers, china markers, towels, scrub brushes and other miscellaneous items

    Processing

    I. Sample screening and set-up: This process is essentially the same for both egg and verm samples (with differences as noted).
    1. Prepare labels corresponding to sample bag identification. Metal strips will work for verms but plastic pot labels can be used for egg samples.
    2. Put on disposable gloves to protect your hands. Open each sample bag, put a screen on top of a dishpan, and push the soil sample through the screen. Mix the screened soil thoroughly.
    3. Fill one pint containers with screened soil and keep label with the soil sample.
    4. Remove all soil particles from the screen and the dishpan (use a brush and towel or whatever it takes) and your hands, otherwise you could contaminate the next sample. Water on the screens attracts dirt or makes mud. If a screen needs washing, wash it and switch to screening soil with another screen.
    5. Start the process again with the next sample (and the next, and the next, and.....). (Note that the samples are run through the elutriator process in sets of four.) When you are done, clean the dishpan and screen (use soap if necessary), and wipe down the sink drain boards. Sweep up any soil that has fallen on the floor.
    II. Running the samples through the elutriator and transferring samples from sieves to tubes (plus a few words on shutting down the elutriator and cleaning up the lab.)
    1. Turning on the elutriator:
      -Turn on the water and attach the air hose to the elutriator.
      -Plug in power cord on elutriator. The front funnels should be in the upright position and sieves set (with the 60 mesh level in the metal funnel support on the collection funnels before you add any soil to the back funnels. Put the 8"-60 mesh sieve on the holder below the separators. Collect liquid from 3 of the 15 tygon tubes.
      -If you are running verms, add the verm holder and tum the motor on (this shakes the verm sieves automatically.
      -When the elutriator finishes rinsing and filling the funnels, it's ready to go.
    2. Make sure the elutriator is clean! (It should already be clean, but..) If necessary, rinse the ledge where you put beakers, the tops and insides of the funnels, and sponge off the side shelves.
    3. At this point, you will need to use different procedures depending on whether you are running egg or verm samples. (If you are running verm + egg samples, you will need to combine the two procedures.) These procedures are described in the next two sections: IIA. Egg samples processing and IIB. Verm sample processing.
    At the end of each processing session, you will need to shut down the elutriator and clean up:

    1. Shutting off the elutriator:
    -Let the elutriator run through its final dump cycle after you are done processing. Then pull out the Dump button, wait until the front funnels come down, rinse the machine thoroughly, and turn the power off. Turn off the motor if you have been running verms.
    -Turn off the water and air.

    2. Cleaning up:
    -Clean the ledges, funnels, funnel rims, and side shelves of the elutriator.
    -Wipe down the sink drain boards.
    -Sweep the floor if you think there is a chance that there is soil on the floor.
    -There should be no sign of soil sitting open to the air on any surface in the room.
    -spray down the metal inside surfaces of the elutriator with shower spray to reduce mineral buildup and mildew

    IIA. Egg sample processing

    1. Rinse all the 20 mesh and 60 mesh sieves before using them. Put a 20 mesh sieve on top of the small funnels in the front of the elutriator to collect debris. Put the sample label in the corresponding funnel. Put the 60 mesh sieve below the sample separators.
    2. Line up your first four samples in correct order on the back of the elutriator. Push the start button, then place each sample into its corresponding large funnel. (Note: do not put the sample into the large funnel before you turn on the elutriator. It will go straight down the drain! The sample will also be lost if the bottom plug does not close off the bottom of the funnel.)
    3. Watch the sieves so that a sample is not lost when the 60 mesh sieve clogs with fine soil particles and overflows losing part of the sample. When the water stops running in the elutriator (approximately 4 minutes) remove the 20 mesh sieves, transfer the label to an empty beaker. Take the 60 mesh sieves containing the SCN cysts and rinse the contents of each sieve into the appropriate beaker corresponding to the sample processed. Then go back, carefully rinse the 20 mesh sieves into the base of the elutriator, and pull out Dump button. When the funnels tip, rinse out the insides of the funnels and the bowls carefully. Then push Dump button back in.
    4. Repeat this step until the contents of all four 60 mesh sieves have been transferred to the beakers. Then rinse all the sieves carefully.
    5. Put the 20 mesh sieves on the receiving funnels, the 60 mesh sieve below the separators and prepare to run another batch of samples. Place the beakers on trays and put in the refrigerator for cyst counts if needed. When all samples have been transferred to the beakers and cysts have been counted, you are ready to grind and stain (see IIIA). If you do not have time to do this, leave the beakers in the refrigerator with a note specifying the sample set number (date) and the words “NOT GROUND.” If you have made a mistake, write a note. —mixed sample ### with sample ###. Make a note on the dry erase board that these samples need to be redone. Another common mistake is putting the sieves on in the wrong order and dumping the cysts down the drain.
    IIB. Verm sample processing.
    1. Before running verm samples through the elutriator, the three tygon tubes leading from the lowermost receiving funnel to the verm sieves must be cleaned. Use soapy water and a long thin bottle brush. These tubes need to be cleaned at least once every day that you run verms. After finishing, pour the rest of the soapy water in the funnels. Then dump the funnels and rinse thoroughly.
    2. Label a set of centrifuge tubes with the sample set number (date). You will also need a water bottle and the small funnel for sample transferring.
    3. Start as for egg samples: Rinse all the sieves carefully (in case someone did not rinse them well after using them the last time). Put a 20 mesh sieve over a 60 mesh sieve on top of the small funnels in the front of the elutriator. (This is if you are doing E & V. If you are only running verms then replace the 20 mesh sieve with a 10 mesh sieve to catch the bigger verms.) Tilt the top (either 10 or 20 mesh) sieve towards the spout so it collects water. Put the large 400 mesh sieves in the holder. Tum on the motor if you haven't already done this (this will automatically shake the sieves). You will not see the shaking of the sieves until you start the elutriator. You still need to carefully watch all screens. The 400 mesh sieve is the most likely to overflow and ruin the sample extraction.
    4. Line up your first four samples in correct order on the back of the elutriator. Push the start button, then put each sample into its corresponding large funnel. (Note: do not pour the sample into the large funnel before you turn on the elutriator-it will go straight down!)
    5. Watch the large sieves while the elutriator is running to make sure they do not overflow. If they start to get full, either turn_ them, tap the sides of the sieves, or spray a little warm water in them. When the water stops running in the elutriator, if you are running E & V, the 60 mesh sieve should be processed as described for egg samples. See below if you are running just verms. Concentrate the soil along one edge of the 400 mesh sieve with the large sprayer hose (gentle spray so you do not blast the nematodes into aerosols), and transfer it to the appropriate centrifuge tube using a wash bottle and the small funnel. Then repeat until the soil collected on each of the four large sieves is in the corresponding centrifuge tube. Rinse the large sieves carefully and set aside. Fingernails ruin the screens. Hold all screens so that you touch the metal holders but not the screens themselves. Some screens are several hundred dollars. Set aside screens that have holes in them as they need to be fixed temporarily and new screens ordered to avoid sample loss or contamination.
    6. If you are running samples for venn determination only, rinse the contents collected on the 1O mesh sieves into the bottom of the elutriator, wash the contents of the 60 mesh sieve into the 400 mesh sieve, wash the contents on the 400 mesh sieve into a centrifuge tube as described above. Rinse the funnels tip, rinse out the insides of the funnels and the bowls carefully. Then push the Dump button back in. Then proceed with rinsing the sieves and dumping as described above.
    7. When all the samples have been elutriated, sieved and collected in centrifuge tubes, you are ready to spin the venns (see IIIB). If you do not have time to do this, leave the tray of centrifuge tubes in the refrigerator with a note specifying the sample set number (date) and the words NOT SPUN.
    Egg Preparation IIIA Grinding and Sieving:

    The egg samples in the beakers contain cysts and the eggs. The cyst walls need to be broken open so we can count the eggs.

    Clean the rubber stopper by running the drill press for 15 seconds using a beaker of clean water. Change the rinse water periodically. The timer is set by moving the hinged hand to the time desired. The timer will tum off at the end of the time. Attach the white collection bucket with the bungie cord if the collection bucket with the drain tube is not in place.

    Take the first beaker and pour the contents of the tube into the pvc tube with the 60 mesh sieve piece. Wet down and then stack a 200 mesh over a 500 mesh sieve and place them under the pvc pipe before starting to grind.

    Grind the sample for 30 seconds adding water either with the sink hose or with a water bottle. The stopper heats up from the friction if liberal amounts of water are not present. Set the timer! When the sample is ground, rinse the stopper with water into the 200 mesh sieve. Gently rinse the sieve to move any attached eggs onto the 500 mesh sieve.

    To make egg counting easier we remove the cyst wall fragments by collecting the fragments on a 200 mesh sieve which allows the eggs to pass through and be caught on the 500 mesh sieve. Discard the cyst wall remains contained on the 200 mesh sieve. The contents of the 500 mesh sieve need to be concentrated in a portion of the edge of the sieve and rinsed into a 150 ml beaker.

    Continue grinding and sieving the rest of the set. Staining: Take the beakers containing the eggs. There should be 20 to 40 ml of liquid containing the eggs in the beakers. Add 1.5 ml acid fucshin egg stain to each beaker. If there is a lot of organic matter in the beakers (dark and dirty looking water) add 2 ml to the beaker. Note: check automatic pipet for correct volume. Put 4 beakers at a time in the microwave oven and heat to just boiling. This depends on the amount of liquid in the beakers but will take about 2 minutes for 4 beakers. The heat sets the stain in the eggs. Proceed with the staining until all beakers containing liquid have been processed. Put the set of beakers in the refrigerator until ready to count.

    Stain recipe - 1:3 ratio of lactic acid to water, 0.035 g acid fucshin/100 ml of liquid. The fans should be turned off before weighing the acid fucshin to prevent stain dust covering unwanted areas.

    IIIB -Spinning Verms

    This step separates the vermiform nematodes from the soil and organic matter making it easier to determine which nematodes are present. It requires running the centrifuge in two steps. Take the venns in the centrifuge tubes into the room with the centrifuge. The centrifuge will hold 16 samples at a time. The centrifuge needs to be balanced. This means that the volume of water in tubes opposite of each other should be equal. If this is not done, it will not only damage the centrifuge but could cause the centrifuge to bounce off the table destroying people and property. Once the centrifuge is loaded (a water blank is used for odd number of samples) set the dial to 5 minutes. There is a hatch mark on the dial for rpm (7.5) and that should not be adjusted. This run will concentrate the nematodes and soil into the bottom of the centrifuge tube. Soon after the centrifuge stops spinning (Do not open the lid of the centrifuge if it is still spinning.) remove the tubes and decant the liquid into the sink. While the centrifuge is on the first spin remove the sugar solution contained in the wash bottle from the refrigerator. Add sugar solution (227 g table sugar/500 ml water) to the centrifuge tubes. Use a spatula and mix the soil from the bottom of the centrifuge tube. Rinse the spatula into the tube just stirred. Avoid contaminating samples. Load the centrifuge making sure the load is balanced. Set the timer for 5 minutes again. While the centrifuge is running, rinse the 500 mesh sieve. Decant the liquid from a centrifuge tube onto the 500 mesh sieve. This size will collect the vermiform nematodes. Rinse the sieve carefully with water. Clean out the centrifuge tube so that no soil remains in the bottom of the tube. Rinse the contents on the 500 mesh sieve into the cleaned centrifuge tube. Continue this process until all the verm samples are cleaned up. Place the rack in the refrigerator for Pat.

    Counting Eggs:

    Take the set of beakers to be counted out of the refrigerator. Add clean water until there is 40 ml of liquid in the beaker to be counted. (If there is already more than 40 ml of liquid in the beaker, dilute the sample to 60 ml and make the appropriate math corrections.) Stir the contents of the beaker with a disposable pipet until the solution is frothy. Withdraw liquid from the suspension using the disposable pipet. Place the liquid in the cavity of the counting slide. The cavity contains 1 ml. Set up the magnification on the microscope such that you can see either the upper and lower lines of one cell starting in the upper left-hand comer of the counting slide. Alternatively you can use less magnification and view 2 cells within the field of view. Move the slide from left to right and then snake down until all cells are counted. Record on the mechanical counter eggs as you view them. Reminder: not all objects stained red in the viewing field are eggs. Eggs are kidney-shaped with smooth edges. Eggshells are empty but retain the general shape of eggs but a slit or only part of the eggshell remains. Count eggs and egg shells. Do not count verms (worm shaped nematodes). Sometimes you will encounter juveniles emerging from eggs and view a verm partially within an egg. Count this as an egg. After the slide has been counted, rinse the contents of the slide into a beaker for disposal with a wash bottle unless told to save the sample. Write down the number of eggs recovered in the sample in the appropriate sample line. The egg count should be converted to 1 pint of soil. Multiply the egg count by 40 for the appropriate amount of liquid in the beaker (here is where you correct for the volume of liquid in the beaker), and multiple by 5 to correct for sampling only from 3 of the 15 tubes. Correct for soil volume if running less than 1 pint.

    At the end of the egg counting session clean the slide with the pipe cleaner. Clean up the counting area when done and leave it ready for the next person to count.

    Processing SCN Samples

    1. Screen the soil so that it is uniform and then return it to its numbered bag.
    2. Measure 8 oz of soil into plastic cup.
    3. On top of the cup of soil, place a nematode bottle cap with a number corresponding to the number of the sample. Place in carrier in numerical order.
    4. Plug in the power cord to USDA's elutriator and then turn on the water.
    5. Set the timer for 3½ minutes.
    6. Turn on the elutriator. Pull out the knob to raise the bowls. (If they do not rise, then check to see that the air pressure is adequate.) Press the trip switch to start a cycle. Let it run a complete cycle to rinse it.
    7. Place 25-mesh sieves onto racks above the bowls and 60-mesh sieves onto racks below the bowls. Press the trip switch, then pour samples into the cones and run the cycle.
    8. Rinse 60-mesh residue into nematode bottle. Place numbered cap onto nematode bottle.
    9. Store in refrigerator until ready to count nematodes. Use square Petri dishes with lines marked on the bottoms.
